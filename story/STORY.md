# Pokemon Unreasonable Edition — Story Bible

## The Region: Absurdia

A crescent-shaped archipelago of mismatched biomes that shouldn't geologically coexist. The region was allegedly "designed by committee" — a council of ancient Pokemon that couldn't agree on anything. The result is a land where a frozen tundra borders a active volcano, a swamp sits on top of a skyscraper district, and nobody finds any of this strange.

Absurdia runs on **Resonance Energy** — a mysterious power source harvested from the emotional bonds between trainers and Pokemon. The entire region's infrastructure (power grid, transportation, hospitals, even the internet) depends on it. This is presented as wholesome by the regional government. It is not.

### Regional Identity

- The region's motto: *"Everything is fine and nothing hurts."*
- Currency is still Pokedollars, but locals call them "Copes"
- The regional Professor is a Pokemon, not a human
- Gym Leaders are elected officials who also run their city's government — poorly
- The Pokemon League doubles as the regional legislature

### Geography (13 Major Areas)

The region is divided into three "Arcs" that correspond to the three acts of the story:

**Arc 1 — "The Tutorial That Refuses to End"**
1. **Nulltown** — Starting town. Population: 7. The town sign reads "Nulltown — We're on the map. Technically." Your house, rival's house, and the Professor's field lab (a barn).
2. **Route N-1 (The Obvious Path)** — A comically over-signposted route. Every 10 steps there's a tutorial NPC. They get increasingly desperate as you ignore them.
3. **Ferro City** — Industrial town built around a steel mill that processes Resonance Energy into usable power. First Gym: STEEL type. The Gym Leader, **Anvil**, is also the city's overworked mayor who hasn't slept in three days.
4. **The Underlake** — An underground lake system connecting Ferro City to the next area. Dark, damp, full of cave Pokemon that are actively annoyed by your presence.
5. **Mirage Meadows** — A beautiful flower field that is 100% artificially maintained by a corporation. Route to second gym. Wild Pokemon here are suspiciously happy.
6. **Prismara Town** — Colorful resort town funded entirely by the Resonance Energy corporation. Second Gym: FAIRY type. Gym Leader **Glint** is a social media influencer who live-streams all gym battles.

**Arc 2 — "Something is Obviously Wrong"**
7. **Route S-1 (The Shortcut)** — A route locals warn you not to take. It's faster. The warnings are warranted.
8. **Ashveil City** — A city perpetually covered in volcanic ash from the nearby Mt. Spite. Third Gym: FIRE type. Gym Leader **Cinder** is a conspiracy theorist who is actually right about everything.
9. **Mt. Spite** — Active volcano. The Resonance Energy corporation has a "research facility" inside it. This is where you first see what they're actually doing.
10. **Gloomharbor** — A port city with a noir atmosphere. Fourth Gym: DARK type. Gym Leader **Murk** is a private detective who investigates crimes the police ignore.
11. **The Rift Shoals** — A chain of islands where reality is slightly broken. Pokemon from other regions appear here with wrong types and unsettling Pokedex entries.

**Arc 3 — "This Was Never About Badges"**
12. **Zenith Plateau** — The Pokemon League and seat of regional government. Fifth through eighth gyms are here in a gauntlet format — the "Zenith Trials." The four Trial Leaders double as the Elite Four.
13. **The Resonance Core** — Hidden final area beneath Zenith Plateau. The heart of the Resonance Energy system. Where the final confrontation takes place.

## Characters

### The Player
A 15-year-old who just moved to Nulltown because their parent got transferred to the Resonance Energy Corporation's new branch office. Unlike vanilla Pokemon protagonists, the player character has *internal monologue* shown in italics during key story beats — dry, sarcastic observations about the absurdity around them.

### The Rival: **Quin**
Your next-door neighbor in Nulltown. Quin is aggressively optimistic and treats the Pokemon journey like a content creator treats a brand deal. They document everything, give everything a "rating out of ten," and refuse to acknowledge anything is wrong with the region even as evidence mounts. Quin is not evil — they're in denial. Their arc is about confronting uncomfortable truths.

- Battle 1: Nulltown (after starter selection)
- Battle 2: Ferro City (before first gym)
- Battle 3: Prismara Town (after second gym)
- Battle 4: Ashveil City (after third gym — tone shifts here, Quin is shaken)
- Battle 5: Gloomharbor (Quin is angry, confrontational)
- Battle 6: Zenith Plateau (Quin has accepted the truth, fights alongside you)
- Battle 7: Post-game rematch (Quin at full strength, friendly rivalry restored)

### The Professor: **Professor Thorn**
A Decidueye wearing a lab coat and reading glasses. Speaks through a translation device built by their former trainer (now deceased). Thorn is the region's leading researcher on Resonance Energy and the only academic willing to publicly question it. Dry, deadpan, occasionally existential. Gives you your starter — not because you're special, but because "someone has to do this, and you were standing closest to the door."

Thorn's Former Trainer is a major lore figure — **Dr. Lumen**, the scientist who originally discovered Resonance Energy and founded the corporation before being pushed out when the board realized they could monetize it. Lumen died under "mysterious circumstances." Thorn has been investigating ever since.

### The Villain Team: **The Optimization Bureau**
Not a traditional evil team — they're a government-corporate hybrid. The Bureau runs the Resonance Energy grid and has slowly taken control of all regional infrastructure. They wear business casual. Their grunts carry briefcases and speak in corporate jargon. Their Pokemon are all Normal-types re-trained to be ruthlessly efficient.

**Their actual plan:** The Bureau discovered that Resonance Energy output increases when the bond between trainer and Pokemon is *stressed* — not strengthened. Fear, desperation, and grief produce more energy than love and trust. They've been engineering situations across the region to maximize emotional distress: releasing aggressive wild Pokemon near towns, rigging gym battles to be traumatic, and suppressing information about Pokemon fainting permanently (the nuzlocke mechanic is *canon* in this story — Pokemon death is real, the Bureau profits from the grief it causes, and they've been covering it up).

**Bureau Leadership:**

- **Director Voss** — The CEO. Calm, rational, genuinely believes they're doing what's necessary to keep the region's power grid running. Not a cackling villain — a utilitarian monster. Uses a team of Pokemon that are powerful but visibly exhausted/unhappy.

- **Vice Director Sera** — Voss's second in command. A true believer in "optimization." Sera is the one who designed the grief-harvesting protocols. More overtly villainous — enjoys the cruelty and hides it behind spreadsheets. Mid-game antagonist.

- **Agent Null** — A Bureau enforcer who keeps appearing to block your progress. Wears a plain grey suit. Never gives their real name. Speaks exclusively in passive voice. ("It has been determined that your progress will be impeded.") Slowly revealed to be a former trainer who lost all their Pokemon to the nuzlocke rules and was recruited by the Bureau in their grief.

### Gym Leaders / Trial Leaders

**Arc 1 Gyms (traditional format):**

1. **Anvil** (Steel) — Ferro City mayor. Exhausted, earnest, trying to keep his city running while the Bureau takes over infrastructure. His gym puzzle involves redirecting power conduits. After you beat him, he quietly asks you to look into what the Bureau is doing at Mt. Spite.

2. **Glint** (Fairy) — Prismara Town influencer. Vapid on the surface, but their Pokemon are unnervingly strong. After the battle, their camera glitches and shows a behind-the-scenes feed where Glint drops the persona entirely: "You're good. Too good to stay in this region. Get out while you can." They don't elaborate.

**Arc 2 Gyms:**

3. **Cinder** (Fire) — Ashveil City conspiracy theorist. Walls covered in string-and-pins boards about the Bureau. Everyone in town thinks he's crazy. He's not. His gym puzzle involves navigating through volcanic vents while avoiding Bureau surveillance drones. After the battle, he gives you a stolen Bureau keycard.

4. **Murk** (Dark) — Gloomharbor detective. Investigating the death of Dr. Lumen. Gym puzzle is a noir-style investigation sequence — you solve a mystery to earn the right to battle. After the battle, Murk shares what they've found: Lumen didn't just die. They were "optimized."

**Arc 3 Zenith Trials (gauntlet — these four also serve as the Elite Four):**

5. **Tempest** (Electric/Flying) — A storm chaser obsessed with raw natural power. Believes the Bureau is suppressing natural weather patterns.
6. **Fossil** (Rock/Ground) — An archaeologist who discovered that Absurdia's weird geography is artificial — the region was terraformed by ancient Pokemon, and the Bureau is disrupting that balance.
7. **Echo** (Psychic/Ghost) — A medium who can communicate with fainted Pokemon. Knows the truth about permadeath. Haunted by it.
8. **Blade** (Dragon/Fighting) — The Champion and de facto head of state. Knows everything. Has done nothing. Your battle with Blade is as much a moral confrontation as a Pokemon battle.

## Plot Summary

### Act 1: "The Tutorial That Refuses to End" (Nulltown -> Prismara Town)

You move to Nulltown. Your parent works for the Resonance Energy Corporation and is excited about the transfer. Professor Thorn gives you a starter (three new starter designs TBD — Grass/Fire/Water as tradition). Quin challenges you immediately. You set out on the standard Pokemon journey.

The first act is *deliberately* conventional. Tutorial NPCs, straightforward routes, friendly rival battles. The humor comes from the player character's internal monologue noticing things that don't add up:
- Why are there so many Bureau employees on Route N-1?
- Why does Ferro City's power plant have military-grade security?
- Why are the wild Pokemon in Mirage Meadows so docile?
- Why did Glint break character after the gym battle?

The act ends with a scene in Prismara Town: you witness a trainer's Pokemon faint in a battle, and the Pokemon doesn't get back up. A Bureau cleanup crew arrives immediately, takes the Pokemon, and tells the trainer "it will be fine." The trainer is escorted away. Nobody in the crowd reacts. Your player character's internal monologue: *"...what just happened?"*

### Act 2: "Something is Obviously Wrong" (Route S-1 -> Gloomharbor)

The tone shifts. The music gets darker. NPCs start saying unsettling things. Quin is still trying to stay positive but is visibly struggling.

Key story beats:
- **The Shortcut (Route S-1):** You encounter wild Pokemon that are far too strong for the area. A Bureau NPC at the entrance warns you not to go this way. A second NPC at the exit says "We told you." This is the first area where the nuzlocke rules can genuinely punish you.

- **Ashveil City / Cinder's Revelation:** Cinder shows you evidence that the Bureau has been artificially inflating wild Pokemon aggression near populated areas. The "random encounters" aren't random. They're engineered.

- **Mt. Spite Infiltration:** Using Cinder's stolen keycard, you enter the Bureau's research facility inside the volcano. You find laboratories where they're measuring Resonance Energy output. The machines spike when nearby trainers lose Pokemon. There are charts. There are projections. There are *quotas*.

- **Gloomharbor / Murk's Investigation:** Murk reveals that Dr. Lumen — Thorn's former trainer — discovered the grief-energy connection and tried to shut the program down. The Bureau had them killed and made it look like a wild Pokemon attack. Thorn receives this information via the player and goes silent for a long time.

- **The Bureau Confrontation (mid-game climax):** Vice Director Sera appears with a squad of agents. She doesn't deny anything. She explains — with charts — why the grief-harvesting is necessary. The region's power grid will collapse without it. Millions of people will lose electricity, heating, medical equipment. "You want to be the person who turns off the lights?" She battles you. Win or lose, she leaves. The moral question lingers.

### Act 3: "This Was Never About Badges" (Zenith Plateau -> The Resonance Core)

You arrive at Zenith Plateau for the League challenge, but the story has outgrown the badge quest. The four Trial Leaders each reveal a piece of the full picture:

- **Tempest** confirms the Bureau controls the weather to drive Pokemon into populated areas
- **Fossil** reveals the region's geography was designed by Arceus-adjacent ancient Pokemon as a self-sustaining ecosystem — the Bureau is strip-mining it
- **Echo** can hear the voices of permanently fainted Pokemon. They are not at peace. They are *powering the grid*.
- **Blade** knew all of this. Blade is the Champion. Blade could have stopped it. Blade chose stability over justice. You battle Blade not for the championship, but to prove that the system must change.

After defeating Blade, the path to the Resonance Core opens. Professor Thorn joins you for the descent. Quin appears — they've been doing their own investigation off-screen and have evidence that could expose the Bureau publicly.

**The Resonance Core:**
- A massive underground facility built around a crystallized nexus of Resonance Energy
- The energy is literally crystallized grief — you can hear faint cries from within the crystals
- Director Voss is waiting. They're not surprised. They explain their position one final time: destroying the Core means the region goes dark. Hospitals shut down. People die. Is your moral high ground worth that?
- You battle Voss. Their Pokemon are strong but clearly suffering.
- After defeating Voss, the choice: destroy the Core or find another way.

**The Resolution:**
Professor Thorn, using Dr. Lumen's original research, reveals a third option: the Core can be *repurposed*. The original Resonance Energy research showed that positive bonds produce energy too — just less efficiently. Thorn proposes rebuilding the grid to run on the slower but sustainable positive energy. It will mean rolling blackouts, rationing, years of rebuilding. But no more death harvesting.

The final scene: you walk out of the Resonance Core into daylight. Quin is broadcasting everything to the region. The Bureau's leadership is arrested. The lights flicker — some go out. But the ones that stay on are powered by something clean.

Credits roll over a montage of the region rebuilding: Anvil finally sleeping, Glint using their platform to spread the truth, Cinder being vindicated, Murk closing the Lumen case.

**Post-game** opens up the Rift Shoals fully, unlocks rematches with all Gym Leaders at full power, and introduces a subplot about the ancient Pokemon that originally built the region returning to help restore the ecosystem.

## Thematic Core

**The central question:** "What are you willing to accept as normal?"

The game starts by presenting standard Pokemon conventions and slowly reveals them to be monstrous when examined honestly. The nuzlocke mechanics aren't just a difficulty mode — they're the story. Pokemon death is real, it matters, and someone is profiting from it. The Bureau isn't doing anything the player hasn't been doing their whole Pokemon career (making creatures fight until they faint). They're just doing it at industrial scale.

The humor comes from the contrast between the bright, colorful Pokemon world and the darkness underneath. NPCs who speak in cheerful Pokemon-game cadence about horrifying things. Tutorial signs that give increasingly unhinged advice. A rival who copes through toxic positivity.

The story respects the player enough to present a villain with a real argument and an ending that doesn't pretend fixing systemic problems is easy.

## Tone Guide

- **Dialog:** Fourth-wall-aware but not fourth-wall-breaking (unlike Littleroot/Oldale's current dialog which is openly meta). Characters know they live in an absurd world; they don't know they're in a game.
- **Humor:** Dark comedy. Gallows humor. Bureaucratic absurdity. Corporate speak as horror.
- **Drama:** Earned through character development, not melodrama. The emotional beats hit harder because the comedy lowers your guard.
- **Player Agency:** The player character has opinions (shown in italics) but the player makes the choices. Internal monologue is observational, not prescriptive.

## Open Design Questions

1. **Starter Pokemon** — Three new designs needed. Should they tie into the Resonance Energy theme? (e.g., a Grass-type that absorbs emotional energy, a Fire-type powered by passion, a Water-type that reflects memories)
2. **New Pokemon** — How many regional variants / new Pokemon for Absurdia? Minimum viable: ~20-30 new designs for key story Pokemon, regional variants for the rest.
3. **Map Count** — 13 major areas will expand to ~60-80 individual maps when interiors, dungeons, and sub-routes are included.
4. **Music** — New compositions or remixed Emerald tracks with tonal shifts?
5. **The Bureau Grunts** — Should they have a uniform typing (Normal) or varied teams that reflect corporate "departments"?
6. **Nuzlocke Integration** — The current nuzlocke system is mechanical. The story makes it diegetic. Do we add dialog that acknowledges Pokemon death in real-time, or keep it implicit?
7. **Post-game Scope** — How much content after the main story? The Rift Shoals could be a substantial post-game area.
